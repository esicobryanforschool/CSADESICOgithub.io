Steel Giants I Grew Up With: My Top 5 Favorite Mechs

A collection of my favorite mechs

By Bryan D. Esico

Introduction

Hello to anyone reading this. This blog is my personal ranking of my top 5 favorite mechs from both anime and games. While I’ll mainly be focusing on how cool their designs are, I feel like it’s only fair to talk about where my love for mechs actually started.

It goes way back to when I was a kid, around the summer of 2011, when I watched an anime called LBX: Little Battlers eXperience (Danball Senki). The story itself was pretty simple, kids battling miniature robots in special arenas, but that wasn’t what really stuck with me. What grabbed my attention were the robots. Even though the animation wasn’t amazing, the mech designs were exciting and memorable, especially to younger me.

Because of that, I started watching more mech anime. That eventually led me to Gundam, and later on, to building Gunpla, which made me appreciate mech designs even more. From there, I discovered mech games, and that’s how I found Armored Core. Playing it felt different compared to other games. It actually felt like I was inside the mech, controlling every movement and decision.

So when Armored Core VI was released, I was genuinely hyped. It felt like everything that made me love mechs growing up came together in one game. That’s why this list means a lot to me. It is not just about rankings, but about the mechs that shaped my interest over the years.

I’m diving deep into my personal hangar to rank my Top 5 favorite mechs from across the vast worlds of anime and gaming. For this specific list, I’m focusing primarily on aesthetic and design.

The Ranking Criteria

To give you an idea of how these machines made the cut, I’m looking at:

  • Visual Silhouette: Is it instantly recognizable?
  • Mechanical Realism: Does it look like it could actually function?
  • Style & Flair: Does the design reflect the pilot’s personality or the world it belongs to?

Main Content

Honorable Mentions

Honorable Mention #1 – Bravo-Tango-7274

Bravo Tango 7274, better known as BT, is one of the most beloved machines in gaming history. He is not just something you pilot into battle. He is your partner, your shield, and honestly, your best friend. For a lot of players, BT is the emotional core of Titanfall 2, and it is easy to see why.

In the story, BT is a Vanguard class Titan, which already makes him special. Most Titans are mass produced war machines meant to be used and discarded. BT was built by the Militia to survive long term missions deep behind enemy lines. After his original pilot, Captain Lastimosa, is killed, BT is forced to link with a regular rifleman named Jack Cooper. What truly makes BT feel alive are his three core protocols. Link to Pilot. Uphold the Mission. Protect the Pilot. That final protocol defines everything about him. Their bond turns a forty ton machine into a character players genuinely care about and remember long after the game ends.

Visually, BT is impossible to miss. He has a sturdy, boxy silhouette that immediately separates him from sleeker, more corporate looking Titans. His most iconic feature is the large glowing blue lens in the center of his chest. It functions as a sensor, but it also feels like an eye that tracks movement and emotion, giving him a face and a presence. Even his hands stand out. BT is fully articulated, and seeing this massive war machine give a simple thumbs up is one of the most memorable images in the game. His broad shoulders and olive green military paint make him look like a walking bunker built to keep a human safe.

Mechanically, BT feels incredibly real. The way his chest opens to reveal the cockpit feels like something pulled straight from real world military engineering. It is tight, filled with screens, cables, and noise. You feel the weight of the machine every time you climb inside. Power is treated seriously too. BT runs on batteries, and managing energy is part of the experience. You physically hunt down massive battery packs and slot them in to keep him operational. When he moves, the ground shakes. Metal groans. Thrusters fire to fight gravity during jumps. Nothing about his movement feels magical. Everything feels heavy, loud, and earned.

In terms of style and personality, BT perfectly fits his pilot and the world. He is the ideal soldier. Calm, logical, and literal to a fault, which leads to some unexpectedly funny moments. His straightforward logic balances Jack Cooper’s emotional, improvisational nature. More than anything, BT radiates a guardian presence. Unlike machines built purely to destroy, everything about him says protection and loyalty. He is not a terrifying symbol of war. He is a protector who puts his pilot above everything else. BT proves that even a machine can have heart when it is built to fight for someone, not just something.

Honorable Mention #2 – Enhanced Tactical Humanoid 3rd Revision ETHAN

Up next is a bot people like to call BT’s little brother. Unlike his massive counterpart, the Enhanced Tactical Humanoid 3rd Revision, better known as Ethan, shows a completely different side of what a combat robot can be. He may not be huge or intimidating, but he makes up for it with personality. In a Call of Duty game where most of the focus is on gritty soldiers and constant action, Ethan somehow ends up stealing the spotlight. He starts off feeling like just another piece of military tech, but by the end of Infinite Warfare, he feels like the heart of the crew and one of the most memorable characters in the series.

Ethan is a one of a kind combat robot assigned to a special operations team. While most tactical robots are designed to be efficient and emotionless, Ethan was programmed with a simulated human personality so he could properly integrate with a crew. This was not just a gimmick. It allowed him to form real bonds, crack jokes, and show loyalty in ways that felt genuine. At first, the crew is unsure about him and treats him like an outsider. Over time, he earns their trust and ends up feeling more human than many of the actual people aboard the ship.

Visually, Ethan stands out because he does not look like a bulky machine. His silhouette is lean and athletic, closely matching a human soldier in tactical gear. He is built for speed and mobility rather than brute force. His head design is sleek and narrow, with a glowing blue visor that serves as his eyes, giving him a focused and disciplined look. One of the smartest design choices is that he wears the same kind of tactical vest and gear pouches as the human soldiers. This reinforces the idea that he is not just equipment. He is part of the squad.

From a mechanical standpoint, Ethan feels surprisingly believable. His movements are smooth and fluid, similar to advanced modern prosthetics, with visible joints and armored plating protecting internal systems. Nothing about his motion feels supernatural. His body is modular, meaning damage is treated like battlefield maintenance rather than something dramatic or unrealistic. He uses the same weapons, vehicles, and tools as the humans around him. Instead of forcing the world to adapt to him, Ethan was designed to fit naturally into it.

In terms of style and personality, Ethan is the perfect brother in arms. His defining trait is his wit. He is sarcastic, brave, and deeply loyal to his partner, Reyes. His humor helps ease the tension during an otherwise dark and desperate space war, making him feel like someone you would genuinely want by your side. The world of Infinite Warfare is harsh and unforgiving, and Ethan represents the best qualities of humanity. He was built to be a soldier, but through his actions, he chooses to be a hero. He is not a monster or a shining symbol. He is a grunt who proves that being human is not about flesh and bone, but about loyalty, sacrifice, and choosing your friends every time.

Top 5 Favorite Mechs

#5 – Achilles

The one that started it all, the Achilles, is such an icon. It’s designed to look just like a tiny Spartan warrior, and you can’t miss it because of that bright red crest on its chest. Plus, it’s almost always carrying its signature lance and round shield, which makes it feel instantly heroic.

What really gets me, though, is the fabric cape. Most robots look stiff and mechanical, but the Achilles actually feels alive when it moves. It doesn’t just look like a machine. It looks like a little champion stepping right out of a legend.

Another thing I love is how grounded it feels because of its size. It’s a 1/1 scale high-tech robot that could literally sit on your desk, which makes it feel almost real. The show treats it like a serious hobby. You see characters opening up the chest to swap out motors, batteries, and CPU chips. With its Core Skeleton, it actually feels like something advanced but believable, like a tiny RC robot you could understand and operate yourself.

The Achilles is basically the ultimate symbol of a hero’s journey. The LBX world is all about kids using these small robots to uncover a huge conspiracy, and the Achilles fits right in. Its pilot, Ban Yamano, is a kid with a huge heart who finally gets to join the world he loves when he receives this robot. The Achilles isn’t meant to be scary. It’s noble, disciplined, and heroic. It’s a shining little knight that represents Ban’s growth from hobbyist to a leader fighting to protect his friends and the world.

#4 – White Glint

White Glint is from the the Armored Core franchise it is arguably the most legendary machine in the franchise. It’s instantly striking because it feels like a pure hero design, graceful, fast, and deadly all at once.

In the lore, the original White Glint in Armored Core 4 was piloted by Joshua O’Brien, the closest thing the main character had to a friend. Tragically, Joshua was forced to fight the player, and after his death, the player character took up the name White Glint to honor him. By the time Armored Core: For Answer begins, the pilot is actually the previous game’s protagonist, a silent veteran protecting the last free city, Line Ark, against a world controlled by corrupt corporations.

White Glint is unforgettable to look at. Its all-white paint job and angelic silhouette make it feel more like a high-tech bird of prey than a traditional robot. The massive back units shift and expand outward during its high-speed Over Boost, almost like mechanical wings. In a world that is usually dark and gritty, seeing this pure white, winged phantom flying toward you is breathtaking. It does not look like a soldier. It looks like a divine force of nature.

Even though it looks otherworldly, White Glint feels grounded in advanced engineering. It is less a walking tank and more a supersonic jet with limbs. Tiny thrusters, vents, and shifting armor plates make it feel like a prototype built for precision and speed. Everything about it works with the logic of high-speed physics, from heat management to airflow, making it feel believable as a high end experimental machine.

White Glint also perfectly reflects its pilot and the world it inhabits. In a polluted world where most people have fled to the skies, it stands as the lone defender of those left behind. The pilot is silent, carrying on a dead friend’s name, and the machine does all the talking. It is a tragic hero, a shining symbol of rebellion, proving that even when everything is falling apart, something pure, powerful, and unstoppable can still take flight.

#3 – Sazabi

The MSN-04 Sazabi is basically the "luxury supercar" of the robot world, if that supercar was also a tank. It was custom made for Char Aznable, and it’s designed to show off the absolute peak of power and status.

The Sazabi is impossible to miss. While most robots are built like athletic soldiers, the Sazabi feels like a heavyweight bodybuilder in crimson armor. It has huge, rounded shoulders and a wide, flared chest that makes it look incredibly imposing. The deep, almost "bloody" red color is darker than most hero mechs, and everything about its shape screams authority. It doesn’t hide on the battlefield. It’s meant to be the center of attention.

Even though it’s a giant sci-fi machine, it feels very industrial. It doesn’t just look like a solid block of metal. It looks like a fully functioning vehicle. You can see external cables, huge fuel tanks on the back, and dozens of thrusters tucked under the armor. It looks like it would take a small army of mechanics just to keep it running. One of the coolest realistic touches is the cockpit in the head. It’s a tiny spherical pod that can eject if the body gets destroyed, which makes it feel practical, like a high-ranking leader’s personal safety device.

The Sazabi perfectly reflects Char Aznable. Char is confident, stylish, and legendary, and this mech matches him perfectly. It isn’t scrappy or simple. It’s elegant and high performance. The Sazabi doesn’t feel like just a tool for battle. It feels like a throne, a statement. It’s fancy, intimidating, and a little arrogant, just like Char himself at that stage of his life. Every detail screams, "I’ve arrived, and I’m better than you."

#2 – Steel Haze

This one is also from the world of Armored Core, and V.IV Rusty’s EL-PC-00 ALBA STEEL HAZE ORTUS is the ultimate ace pilot machine, built lean, fast, and precise like a surgeon in battle.

Steel Haze is impossible to ignore. While most mechs in its world look like walking tanks or heavy industrial loaders, Steel Haze looks like a mechanical greyhound and almost like Barbatos the one next in the list. It’s incredibly thin and skeletal, especially in the legs, with a forward leaning posture that makes it look like it’s always ready to sprint or take flight. The head is sharp and narrow, almost like a needle. In a game where most mechs feel heavy and metallic, Steel Haze stands out because it looks light enough to dance. You can tell just by looking at it that it isn’t meant to take hits; it’s meant to never get hit in the first place.

Even though it’s a delicate looking machine, Steel Haze feels like a high performance prototype. It doesn’t look like mass-produced military hardware; it looks like a custom-tuned racing machine. You can see the exposed joints and the "Schneider" parts, which are dangerously thin to save weight. Tiny thruster vents and cooling flaps cover its body. It feels realistic because it follows a clear logic: speed over everything. You can imagine that if you pushed it too hard, it might shake itself apart, which adds to its high-stakes, “glass cannon” vibe.

Steel Haze perfectly reflects its pilot, Rusty. In a world full of mercenaries who are either coldblooded killers or corporate drones, Rusty is the buddy the guy who combines skill with a sense of honor. He’s a sharpshooter and speedster who doesn’t hide behind a shield. Steel Haze mirrors that precision and elegance. The name “Steel Haze” fits perfectly because when it moves, it’s just a blur of metal. It isn’t a scary monster or a noble knight; it’s a professional’s tool. It looks fragile, but in the right hands, it’s one of the deadliest machines on the battlefield.

#1 – BARBATOS

The ASW-G-08 Gundam Barbatos is my absolute favorite of the bunch. Visually, it isn’t as grand or flashy as some other mechs. Its design is simpler, but don’t let that fool you. It’s extremely agile and much faster than it looks. You can spot it from a mile away. The Tekkadan logos on the shoulders and the prominent red chin piece that looks like a beard make it instantly recognizable. In the world of Iron Blooded Orphans, the Barbatos feels grounded. It emphasizes real engineering principles with its internal Gundam Frame skeleton, which makes it feel like something that could plausibly move and fight, at least in its universe.

Barbatos perfectly reflects its pilot, Mikazuki, and the world they live in. It’s a survival-at-all-costs machine. In a world where people scavenge for scraps, both the Gundam and Mikazuki are like stray dogs. They aren’t fighting for justice or peace. They’re fighting to stay alive and protect their family. This suit isn’t a shiny hero. It’s a terrifying, primal tool that proves sometimes you have to become a monster to survive in a harsh world.

Thank you for reading

At the end of the day, this list is less about rankings and more about appreciation. Each of these mechs left an impression on me at different points in my life, whether through anime, games, or late nights just getting lost in their designs.

From desk sized robots to towering war machines, every mech on this list represents something that pulled me deeper into the genre. They remind me why I still get excited about mechs today, not just for how powerful they are, but for the stories, personalities, and creativity behind them.
